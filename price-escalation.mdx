## Overview

Enterprise contracts often include pre-agreed price increases — a 5% annual uplift, or a jump to a fixed price in year two. Until now, applying these changes meant manually editing a charge when the date arrived, with no audit trail explaining why the rate changed.

Price Escalation lets you schedule these increases in advance, directly on a charge or plan feature, so they execute automatically — and still go through the same maker-checker approval as any other billing change.

## How it works

Escalations can be set at the **charge level** or the **plan feature level**, and support four types:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Flat %">Increases the rate by a fixed percentage.</Card>
  <Card title="Flat Amount">Increases the rate by a fixed absolute value.</Card>
  <Card title="Tier-based">Applies the escalation independently within each pricing tier.</Card>
  <Card title="Target Price">Moves the rate directly to a specified value on the escalation date.</Card>
</CardGroup>

Each escalation also has a **basis**, which determines how repeated escalations compound:

- **Simple** — always calculated from the original rate
- **Compounding** — calculated from the last escalated rate, so increases build on each other over time

## Governance built in

When an escalation date arrives, ZenStatement doesn't change the rate silently. It generates a pending rate change that a checker must approve — the same governance flow used for any other charge update. Once approved, the new rate applies going forward; past invoices are untouched.

If a checker rejects the change, the charge reverts to its prior rate and the maker is notified with the reason.

## Hierarchy-aware by default

If a parent customer's plan includes an escalation, child entities inherit it automatically — unless an override is explicitly set at the child level. You don't need to configure the same escalation multiple times across a hierarchy.

## Full audit trail

Every escalation is logged in the Audit Trail: the original rate, the new rate, the type and basis used, who configured it, who approved it, and when it took effect.

<Tip>
  No competitor in this space supports native price escalations — most require manual contract amendments or external spreadsheets to track scheduled increases. Price Escalation makes this a governed, native capability.
</Tip>
